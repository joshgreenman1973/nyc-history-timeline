Sources & method

How this record was built, and where every date comes from.

Approach

This is a curated selection of turning points in the history of New York City — not a comprehensive chronicle. Events were chosen to span the full arc of the city, from the Lenape homeland of Lenapehoking through Dutch New Amsterdam, English colonial rule, the Revolution, the 19th-century boom, the 1898 consolidation of the five boroughs, and the modern metropolis.

Each event was checked against reputable references. Every card carries a direct link to a source — encyclopedic, governmental, archival or scholarly — so you can verify the claim yourself. Dates that are approximate or contested are marked c. and flagged with a "medium" confidence label.

Editorial notes

Descriptions aim to state facts without editorializing. Where a date is traditionally given but debated by historians, the traditional date is used and noted. This project favors clarity over completeness: many worthy moments were left out to keep the story legible.

Layers (open a card with "Read more")

Many cards go deeper. A ▣ primary source links the actual original document (a 1609 ship's journal, a colonial charter, a period court record, a statute), each fetched and confirmed to support the claim. A Historian's lens gives a named scholar's argument about the moment. Further reading points to an acclaimed narrative history. In fiction lists films, TV, novels and plays set in the event or period. Attributions in every layer were independently verified.

Images

Card images are genuine period images — paintings, engravings, maps and photographs made in or near the event's own era (the 1664 Duke's Plan, a Currier & Ives view of the Brooklyn Bridge, Library of Congress photographs), rendered as halftone. Each was picture-edited against Wikimedia Commons to remove anachronistic modern photos; where no period image exists, the card deliberately shows none. Images are hosted by Wikimedia Commons under their own licenses and credited in the corner of each card.
